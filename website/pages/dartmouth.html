Dartmouth Conference

📅 1956 👤 John McCarthy et al. 📍 Dartmouth College 🏷️ Event

Overview

In the summer of 1956, John McCarthy organized the Dartmouth Summer Research Project on Artificial Intelligence — a two-month workshop that gave the field its name. McCarthy, along with Marvin Minsky, Claude Shannon, and Nathaniel Rochester, proposed that "every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can in principle be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it."

The workshop brought together the researchers who would define AI for decades: Allen Newell and Herbert Simon demonstrated the Logic Theorist, the first AI program. The attendees split into two camps — those pursuing symbolic AI (logic, search, rules) and those interested in neural networks and learning.

Though the workshop didn't produce major breakthroughs, it established AI as a distinct field of research and set the agenda for the next two decades. The optimism was immense — many believed human-level AI was just years away.
