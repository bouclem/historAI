Minsky & Papert's "Perceptrons"

📅 1969 👤 Marvin Minsky & Seymour Papert 📍 MIT 🏷️ Event / Critique

Overview

In 1969, Marvin Minsky and Seymour Papert published "Perceptrons" — a book that mathematically analyzed the capabilities and limitations of single-layer perceptrons. Their central result: a single-layer perceptron cannot learn the XOR function, or any pattern that is not linearly separable.

The book was rigorous and correct. However, it was widely misinterpreted as a condemnation of neural networks in general. In reality, Minsky and Papert knew that multilayer networks could solve XOR and other non-linear problems — but no effective training algorithm for multilayer networks existed yet (backpropagation wouldn't be popularized until 1986).

The impact was devastating. Funding for neural network research collapsed. The field entered what became known as the "AI winter" — a period lasting over a decade where neural network research was effectively frozen out of mainstream AI. Researchers pivoted to symbolic AI, expert systems, and other approaches.

It wasn't until the 1980s, with the popularization of backpropagation by Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams, that neural network research experienced a resurgence. The perceptron's core idea — learning by adjusting weights based on errors — survived and became the foundation of modern deep learning.
