Pre-Trained Perceptron

📅 1974 👤 historAI Project 🏷️ Divergence — Neutral

Overview

The Pre-Trained Perceptron is a divergence architecture — transfer learning, decades before the concept had a name. The standard perceptron initializes weights to zero and learns from scratch. This variant starts with weights learned on a previous (source) task and fine-tunes them on the target task.

The intuition is simple: if the source and target tasks share structure — similar features, related decision boundaries — the pre-trained weights provide a better starting point than zeros. Fewer updates are needed, leading to faster convergence. When the tasks are unrelated, the pre-trained weights can be worse than random initialization, and the perceptron must "unlearn" before it can learn.

The learning rule is identical to the standard perceptron:

w_i(t+1) = w_i(t) + r · (d_j − y_j) · x_j,i

The only difference is the initial condition: w(0) = w_pretrained instead of w(0) = 0.

This could have been explored in 1974, during the AI winter, when researchers were looking for ways to make perceptrons more efficient with limited compute. Transfer learning wasn't formally studied until the 1990s, and pre-training became prominent with deep belief networks in 2006.

Benchmark results: The pre-trained perceptron matches the standard perceptron on accuracy across all datasets. Its advantage is in convergence speed: on linearly separable data, it converges in 1 epoch instead of 2 — the pre-trained weights are already close to a good solution. On non-linearly separable datasets, it shows a marginal improvement on Extended XOR (50.5% vs 48.0%). The divergence status is Neutral: same accuracy, faster convergence — a real but modest benefit.
