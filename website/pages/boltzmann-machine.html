Boltzmann Machine

📅 1985 👤 Ackley, Hinton & Sejnowski 🏷️ Real History

Overview

The Boltzmann Machine is a stochastic neural network with visible and hidden units, symmetric connections, and an energy function inspired by statistical physics. Published by Ackley, Hinton, and Sejnowski in 1985, it extends the Hopfield Network's energy-based approach with hidden units and probabilistic (Bernoulli) neurons.

Each unit is binary (0 or 1) and turns on with probability:

P(unit_i = 1) = sigmoid(Σ_j W_ij · unit_j + b_i)

Training uses Contrastive Divergence (CD-1) — a simplified approximation of maximum likelihood learning. The network alternates between a "positive phase" (clamping visible units to real data and sampling hidden units) and a "negative phase" (free-running Gibbs sampling to generate fantasy data). The weight update is the difference between positive and negative associations.

For classification, we train the BM as an unsupervised feature extractor, then train a linear classifier on the learned hidden representations — the same strategy later used in Deep Belief Networks (2006).

The Boltzmann Machine is the direct ancestor of Restricted Boltzmann Machines (RBMs), Deep Belief Networks, and modern energy-based models.

Benchmark results: The Boltzmann Machine is surprisingly strong — it matches the perceptron at 100% on linearly separable data, high-dimensional data (10D), and large-scale data (1000 samples). On noisy data it achieves 59.4% (vs 82% for perceptron) — the binarization of inputs loses information. On non-linear datasets (circles, XOR) it hovers around 50%. The stochastic training is slower than deterministic methods but the learned features are robust.
