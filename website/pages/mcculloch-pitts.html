McCulloch & Pitts Neuron

📅 1943 👤 Warren McCulloch & Walter Pitts 📍 University of Illinois / MIT 🏷️ Theoretical Model

Overview

In 1943, Warren McCulloch (a neurophysiologist) and Walter Pitts (a logician) published "A Logical Calculus of the Ideas Immanent in Nervous Activity" — a paper that would become the theoretical foundation of all neural networks.

They proposed a simple model: a neuron fires if the sum of its inputs exceeds a threshold. By connecting these threshold units, they showed that networks of artificial neurons can implement any Boolean function — AND, OR, NOT, XOR. This was the first proof that neuron-like elements can perform computation.

Their model was inspired by Turing's work on computation and by the structure of biological neurons. Though they didn't implement a learning algorithm (the McCulloch-Pitts neuron has fixed weights), their insight that neurons can compute logical functions directly inspired Rosenblatt's perceptron 14 years later.

The Model

A McCulloch-Pitts neuron takes binary inputs and produces a binary output:

y = 1 if Σ(x_i · w_i) ≥ θ, else 0

Where w_i are fixed weights (1 for excitatory, -1 for inhibitory) and θ is the threshold. The neuron fires when the weighted sum of inputs reaches the threshold.
