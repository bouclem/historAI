Second AI Winter (1987–1993)

📅 1987–1993 👤 Multiple actors 🏷️ Event

Overview

The Second AI Winter was a period of commercial collapse and funding cuts that lasted roughly from 1987 to 1993. Unlike the first winter, which was driven by academic criticism and government report, the second was primarily a commercial failure — the expert systems market, worth hundreds of millions of dollars, collapsed almost overnight.

The Expert Systems Boom

After the first AI Winter, optimism returned in the early 1980s with the rise of expert systems — programs that encoded human expert knowledge as if-then rules. Companies like Digital Equipment Corporation used expert systems (XCON) to configure computers, reportedly saving $40 million annually. The promise was captured in the slogan "in the knowledge lies the power."

Specialized LISP machines — workstations designed specifically for AI development — became a booming industry. Companies like Symbolics, Lisp Machines Inc., and Texas Instruments sold machines costing $50,000–$100,000 each. By 1987, the LISP machine market was worth half a billion dollars.

Government funding surged. DARPA's Strategic Computing Initiative (1983) poured $100 million into AI research by 1985, funding 92 projects at 60 institutions. Japan's Fifth Generation Computer Project launched with similar ambitions. The field was booming — and overpromising again.

The Collapse

The LISP machine market collapsed in 1987. General-purpose workstations from Sun Microsystems, and increasingly powerful desktop computers from Apple and IBM, made expensive specialized AI hardware obsolete. An entire industry worth half a billion dollars was replaced in a single year. Symbolics, once valued at over $200 million, saw its stock collapse from $77 per share to under $3. Lisp Machines Inc. filed for bankruptcy in 1989.

Expert systems proved brittle. They worked in narrow domains but failed to generalize. The knowledge-engineering bottleneck — the laborious process of extracting rules from human experts — made them expensive to build and maintain. When conditions changed even slightly, the systems broke. They couldn't learn from new data; every rule had to be hand-coded.

DARPA pulled out. In 1987, Jack Schwarz took over DARPA's Information Processing Technology Office and dismissed expert systems as "clever programming." He cut AI funding "deeply and brutally," eviscerating the Strategic Computing Initiative. The flagship autonomous land vehicle project was abandoned. DARPA redirected its focus toward high-performance computing and "grand challenges" — not AI.

The Fifth Generation Project fizzled. Japan's ambitious attempt to build a next-generation AI computer produced no breakthroughs. The project was quietly wound down, reinforcing the perception that AI had overpromised again.

Impact and Legacy

Hundreds of AI companies failed. Research funding collapsed. The term "artificial intelligence" became toxic — researchers rebranded their work as "machine learning," "pattern recognition," or "data mining" to avoid the stigma. Neural network research, which had been experiencing a quiet resurgence thanks to backpropagation (1986), was caught in the downdraft despite being unrelated to the expert systems failure.

Yet the winter had a silver lining. With symbolic AI in retreat, statistical and connectionist approaches gained ground. Researchers like Yann LeCun (convolutional networks, 1989) and Vladimir Vapnik (support vector machines, 1995) developed the methods that would eventually power modern AI. The winter forced the field to focus on rigorous, measurable results rather than grand promises — a lesson that would prove invaluable when deep learning finally arrived.
