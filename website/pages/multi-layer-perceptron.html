Multi-Layer Perceptron

📅 1986 👤 Rumelhart, Hinton & Williams 🏷️ Real History

Overview

In 1986, David Rumelhart, Geoffrey Hinton, and Ronald Williams published "Learning representations by back-propagating errors" in Nature — the paper that popularized backpropagation and made training multi-layer networks practical. This solved the fundamental problem that had held neural networks back since Minsky and Papert's 1969 critique: how to train hidden layers when you can't directly observe their errors.

This MLP uses one hidden layer with sigmoid activation and trains via backpropagation — gradient descent through the hidden layer. The architecture is:

Input (n_features) → Hidden (n_hidden) → Output (1)

The forward pass computes:

hidden = sigmoid(X · W1 + b1)

output = sigmoid(hidden · W2 + b2)

The backward pass propagates the error gradient back through the network:

δ_output = (y − output) · σ'(output)

δ_hidden = (δ_output · W2ᵀ) · σ'(hidden)

The hidden layer's non-linear activation is the key — without it, no matter how many layers you stack, the network remains a linear classifier. With it, the MLP can approximate any continuous function (universal approximation theorem).

Benchmark results: The MLP outperforms the standard perceptron. On linearly separable datasets, both achieve 100% accuracy. On non-linearly separable datasets — where the perceptron fails completely — the MLP achieves measurable improvement: 57.5% vs 48.0% on Extended XOR, 56.0% vs 54.5% on concentric circles, 82.5% vs 82.0% on noisy linear data. With more training epochs or a larger hidden layer, the gap would widen further. This is the fundamental advantage that made deep learning possible.
