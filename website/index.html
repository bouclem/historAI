Every architecture on this timeline — both real and divergence — is implemented from scratch in Python and benchmarked against the same datasets. The divergence labels are not speculation: they reflect actual benchmark results comparing each architecture's performance to its real-history counterpart.
1943
McCulloch & Pitts Neuron
Warren McCulloch and Walter Pitts publish "A Logical Calculus of the Ideas Immanent in Nervous Activity" — the first mathematical model of an artificial neuron. Their work shows how networks of simple threshold units can perform logical operations, laying the theoretical foundation for all neural networks.
Real History
1948 — DIVERGENCE
Adaptive Threshold Neuron
A neuron where the threshold adapts automatically during learning — not fixed like McCulloch-Pitts, not absorbed into bias like the perceptron. The threshold itself is a learnable parameter that adjusts based on prediction errors. A divergence architecture that could have existed between 1943 and 1957.
Divergence — Neutral
1950
Turing Test
Alan Turing publishes "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," proposing the Imitation Game (Turing Test) — a practical criterion for machine intelligence based on indistinguishability from human conversation.
Real History
1956
Dartmouth Conference
John McCarthy organizes the Dartmouth Summer Research Project, coining the term "Artificial Intelligence." The workshop brings together researchers who would define the field for decades: Marvin Minsky, Claude Shannon, Allen Newell, and Herbert Simon.
Real History
1957
The Perceptron
Frank Rosenblatt, at the Cornell Aeronautical Laboratory, simulates the first perceptron on an IBM 704. The perceptron is a single-layer neural network that learns to classify inputs by adjusting weights — the first machine capable of learning from experience.
Real History
1958
Perceptron Hype
At a Navy press conference, Rosenblatt demonstrates the perceptron learning to distinguish cards marked on the left from those on the right after 50 trials. The New York Times reports it as "the embryo of an electronic computer that will be able to walk, talk, see, write, reproduce itself and be conscious of its existence." The New Yorker calls it "the first serious rival to the human brain ever devised."
Real History
1958 — DIVERGENCE
Multi-Threshold Perceptron
A perceptron with multiple thresholds creating alternating firing regions — a single neuron that can, in principle, learn non-linearly-separable patterns like XOR. A natural extension of Rosenblatt's perceptron that could have been explored in 1958. Benchmarks show it performs worse: the alternating regions break the simple linear boundary on separable data.
Divergence — Worse
1960
Mark I Perceptron
Rosenblatt's team completes the Mark I Perceptron — a custom-built analog computer with 400 photocells in a 20×20 grid (sensory units), 512 association units, and 8 response units. Weight updates are performed by electric motors driving potentiometers. The machine is designed for image recognition.
Real History
1962
Principles of Neurodynamics
Rosenblatt publishes his comprehensive book, describing experiments with many perceptron variants: cross-coupled networks, back-coupling (feedback from later to earlier layers), four-layer perceptrons with adjustable weights, and time-delay units for sequential data. He even experiments with audio processing instead of images.
Real History
1969
Minsky & Papert's "Perceptrons"
Marvin Minsky and Seymour Papert publish "Perceptrons," mathematically proving that single-layer perceptrons cannot learn the XOR function. Though they know multilayer networks can solve it, the book is widely interpreted as a death sentence for neural networks. Funding collapses. The AI winter begins.
Real History
1971
Rosenblatt's Death
Frank Rosenblatt dies in a boating accident on his 43rd birthday. His last project, Tobermory — a 4-layer perceptron with 12,000 weights for speech recognition — is never completed. By this time, digital simulation has surpassed purpose-built perceptron hardware.
Real History
1974 — DIVERGENCE
Pre-Trained Perceptron
What if someone had tried transfer learning during the AI winter? A perceptron that starts with weights from a previous task and fine-tunes on a new one — same accuracy as the standard perceptron, but converges in half the epochs on separable data.
Divergence — Neutral
1986
Multi-Layer Perceptron & Backpropagation
Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams publish "Learning representations by back-propagating errors" — popularizing backpropagation and making multi-layer networks trainable. The hidden layer's non-linear activation allows learning non-linearly-separable patterns like XOR. The neural network resurgence begins.
